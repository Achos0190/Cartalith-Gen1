Tides: the moons' gravitational pull (∝ mass ÷ distance³, ÷ g) sets the open-ocean tidal range; it amplifies in shallow shelf seas and funnels at coasts into a spring tidal-range field — wide intertidal flats and "wandering flood zones" hugging gentle coasts (coastal hazard for settlements/routes; exported as tidal_range.f32). Preview in the Tides debug view.
Surface gravity scales fluvial & glacial erosion (×g), temperature lapse (×g), crater size (×g−0.22), wave energy (×1/g) and rescales peak altitude (~1/g). Talus angle stays fixed: friction-dominated, gravity-independent. Geoid makes sea level a field, not a number: a fast-spinning planet's seas bulge at the equator (J2 ∝ Ω²R/g) on top of lumpy-mass harmonics and mantle noise — coastlines, climate ocean masks and biomes all follow the local sea level. Preview it in the Geoid debug view before enabling. Day length and tilt also drive the weather model (circulation cells, seasons).

Temperature falls with latitude (insolation) and altitude (lapse rate). In Whole world mode the map spans pole-to-pole; in Region mode you set the latitude band. Carves dendritic river valleys into the terrain by fluvial incision (implicit stream-power, Braun & Willett 2013) with multiple-flow-direction drainage so channels follow the real slope (no diagonal artefacts). Uplift is off by default — raise it only to grow mountain ranges that compete with incision (tectonically active orogens); at 0 it purely carves existing relief. Channeling sets how aggressively rivers incise. Deposition refills valleys with alluvium and builds deltas, but can never raise a channel above the surrounding land. Rain → erosion makes wet regions carve faster. Switch to the Flow view to see the rivers.
5
Evolve couples climate and erosion: each cycle carves with stream-power, rebounds isostatically, then recomputes the weather (temperature, winds, rainfall) on the freshly carved terrain — so rain shadows and river networks migrate as the mountains grow and wear down, instead of being frozen from generation. Set Uplift > 0 first for a tectonically active orogen that grows while it erodes.

Carves high, cold terrain (above the snowline) into U-shaped valleys. Ice follows the drainage network and widens valleys laterally instead of just cutting down. Glacier heads scoop into cirque bowls. Lower the snowline to glaciate more of the landscape; raise it to restrict ice to the peaks. Run after erosion for best results — erode first, then glaciate.

Three coastal processes in one pass. Sea cliffs: wave energy erodes exposed coastal cells and leaves steep cliff faces (run multiple times to carve retreating cliffs). Estuaries: high-flow river mouths near sea level are widened with an exponential taper — funnel-shaped inlets emerge where major rivers meet the coast. Save & export

PNG keeps full detail — pick a size, then export.
0%
One .zip with everything: the rendered map (PNG), raw 32-bit heightmap + temperature + rainfall, each layer as an image, and all parameters. Load restores the full world.
Region refine — high-res tiles from a map selection
Drag a rectangle on the map, then Refine & export: each tile is amplified from the coarse world in world-space coordinates (seam-free by construction), packed as 16-bit height + rendered PNG, with a schema-2 manifest. Set the tile grid (X×Y) and the per-tile resolution independently — tile pixels follow the selection's true shape, so non-square regions are never squished. 4×4 at 4096 px = a 16k region without ever allocating 16k at once.

Performance

—
Experimental. Heavy grid passes (currently thermal erosion) run on your GPU when it's supported, and fall back to the CPU automatically. On startup the GPU path is checked against the CPU on a small test grid; if the results don't match, or your device lacks WebGL 2 float support, it stays on CPU. Untick to force CPU. More passes (weather, diffusion, stream-power) will move to the GPU as they're validated.
