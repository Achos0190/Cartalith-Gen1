Moisture evaporates off the seas (more from warm water), rides the wind, and rains where air climbs terrain or saturates — windward coasts wet, lee interiors dry. Stream-power couples tectonic uplift with fluvial erosion (Cordonnier 2016): it grows ranges and carves dendritic river networks. Deposition routes eroded sediment downhill — it deposits where the gradient drops (alluvial fans) and at the coastline (deltas). Rain → erosion makes wet areas erode faster and develop denser drainage (tropical weathering feedback); at 0 erosion is uniform. Switch to the Flow view to see the rivers.

Glacial erosion

Carves high, cold terrain (above the snowline) into U-shaped valleys. Ice follows the drainage network and widens valleys laterally instead of just cutting down. Glacier heads scoop into cirque bowls. Lower the snowline to glaciate more of the landscape; raise it to restrict ice to the peaks. Run after erosion for best results — erode first, then glaciate. Rivers overlay the drainage network as blue channels on the biome view.

Three coastal processes in one pass. Sea cliffs: wave energy erodes exposed coastal cells and leaves steep cliff faces (run multiple times to carve retreating cliffs). Estuaries: high-flow river mouths near sea level are widened with an exponential taper — funnel-shaped inlets emerge where major rivers meet the coast. Tidal marsh: the narrow, flat intertidal band is roughened with organic micro-relief. Run after stream-power carve so river mouths are established.
